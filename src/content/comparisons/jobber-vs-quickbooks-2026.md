<p><strong>Jobber is field service management software. QuickBooks is accounting software. They don't really compete. Jobber runs $49-$249/month and handles scheduling, dispatching, quoting, and invoicing. QuickBooks runs $35-$235/month and handles your books, taxes, and payroll. Most serious field service businesses use both.</strong></p>

<h2>What You're Actually Comparing</h2>

<p><strong>Jobber is purpose-built for field service operations: quoting jobs, scheduling crews, dispatching techs, and invoicing from the field. QuickBooks is general accounting software adapted for small businesses. Comparing them is fair only if you understand they're designed for different parts of your business. The overlap is invoicing. That's about it.</strong></p>

<p>This comparison comes up constantly in field service forums. Contractors wonder: do I actually need Jobber if I already pay for QuickBooks? Can QuickBooks handle scheduling? Can Jobber replace my accountant's favorite tool?</p>

<p>The answer is usually "use both," but that's not always practical when you're watching every dollar. So let's get specific about what each one does, what it costs, and when you can reasonably skip one.</p>

<p>Jobber is FSM software built for trades: HVAC, plumbing, electrical, landscaping, pest control, cleaning, general contracting. It manages the full job cycle from first contact to final payment. If you run a crew of any size, your day-to-day operations live in Jobber.</p>

<p>QuickBooks Online is accounting software used by millions of small businesses across every industry. It's excellent at tracking money: income, expenses, payroll, taxes, bank reconciliation. It can generate invoices and has some basic job tracking. But it was not built to dispatch a three-truck plumbing crew, and trying to force that use case out of it is a genuinely painful experience.</p>

<h2>Where Jobber Wins</h2>

<p><strong>Jobber wins at everything that happens between when the customer calls and when the invoice is paid. Scheduling, dispatching, route optimization, automated follow-ups, online booking, and field-level job management. These are the functions that determine how much work your team can actually push through in a day.</strong></p>

<h3>Scheduling and Dispatch</h3>

<p>If you're running a three-truck HVAC crew, this is where QuickBooks completely falls flat. Jobber gives you a drag-and-drop calendar, real-time GPS on each tech, and the ability to reassign jobs mid-day when something runs long. Managing this in QuickBooks is not a real option. You'd be back to a whiteboard or a spreadsheet, which puts a hard ceiling on how efficiently you can run the day.</p>

<h3>Client Management</h3>

<p>Jobber keeps a complete history for every client: every quote, every job, every invoice, every note, every photo from the site. When a customer calls back six months later about that water heater install, you pull it up in ten seconds. QuickBooks can show you an invoice history. That's not the same thing and it's not close.</p>

<h3>Mobile App</h3>

<p>The Jobber mobile app is legitimately good. Techs see their daily schedule, update job status, attach site photos, get customer signatures on-screen, and send invoices before they're back in the van. QuickBooks has a mobile app, but it's built for reviewing financials and recording expenses, not for managing a job site. The two are not comparable experiences.</p>

<h3>Automated Communications</h3>

<p>Starting on the Connect plan ($129/mo), Jobber automatically sends quote follow-ups, appointment reminders, and post-job review requests without anyone lifting a finger. For a shop running 20 or more jobs a week, that automation saves your office person three to four hours of follow-up calls. The review requests alone have a measurable impact on Google review counts, which directly affects local search ranking for every trade that depends on it.</p>

<h3>Online Booking</h3>

<p>The Grow plan includes a booking widget you can drop on your website. Customers pick a time slot that fits your schedule, it flows directly into Jobber, and your team gets notified. If you're getting steady web traffic, this can generate a few extra jobs per week without any manual intake work. For a solo plumber or electrician doing 8-10 calls a day, that's real money. You can see <a href="/reviews/jobber-review-2026">how the full Jobber platform breaks down across all three plans</a> if you want to dig deeper before committing.</p>

<h2>Where QuickBooks Wins</h2>

<p><strong>QuickBooks wins on the financial side of your business. Bank reconciliation, payroll processing, tax prep, detailed P&amp;L reports, and accountant access. If you're doing real bookkeeping and not just invoicing, there's no FSM software that replaces QuickBooks for this work.</strong></p>

<h3>Your Accountant Wants QuickBooks</h3>

<p>This is a practical reality. Most accountants and bookkeepers work in QuickBooks. When tax season hits, handing them a Jobber export is not the same as clean books in QBO. If you're serious about your financials, and once you're past $200k in revenue you should be, QuickBooks for the accounting side is not optional. Fighting this is a losing battle.</p>

<h3>Bank Reconciliation and Expense Tracking</h3>

<p>Connect your bank accounts and credit cards to QuickBooks and it automatically imports every transaction, suggests categories, and lets you reconcile against your statements. This is how you catch billing errors, track job costs accurately, and know your actual profit margins. Jobber shows you what you invoiced. QuickBooks shows you what you actually made after expenses. Those are very different numbers and you need both.</p>

<h3>Payroll</h3>

<p>If you have W2 employees, you need payroll software. QuickBooks Payroll is a natural add-on if you're already in QBO. It handles tax withholding, direct deposit, state filings, and W2s at year end. Jobber tracks time but does not process payroll. For any shop running employees, this gap in Jobber is not a minor inconvenience, it's a hard stop.</p>

<h3>Financial Reporting</h3>

<p>QuickBooks generates proper financial reports: profit and loss, balance sheets, cash flow statements, accounts receivable aging. Jobber has some revenue reporting, but it's job-level data, not business-level accounting. When you sit down with a bank to get a business loan or a line of credit, they want QuickBooks-level financials. Jobber exports won't cut it in that conversation.</p>

<h2>Who Should Pick Which (Or Both)</h2>

<p><strong>Any field service business with more than one truck, or more than 10 jobs per week, should be using both Jobber and QuickBooks. The only case for using just one is a brand-new solo operator still figuring out volume. In that case, start with Jobber and add QuickBooks within the first six months.</strong></p>

<h3>Solo Contractor Under 5 Jobs Per Week</h3>

<p>You can get by with QuickBooks Simple Start ($35/mo) while you're building. Use Google Calendar for scheduling. Track jobs in a spreadsheet. This works until it doesn't, and it will eventually break down on you. Add Jobber Core ($49/mo) once you're consistently booking 10 or more jobs a week and spending more than two hours a week on admin. The $84/mo combined setup is the right call before you hit that wall.</p>

<p>This is where Jobber becomes mandatory. Dispatching a multi-truck crew without real scheduling software is how you miss jobs, double-book techs, and lose customers to whoever answers faster. Jobber Connect ($129/mo) plus QuickBooks Essentials ($65/mo) is $194/mo total. That's less than one lost job per month for most trades. The math is easy.</p>

<h3>If You Absolutely Must Pick One</h3>

<p>Pick Jobber. You can export invoices and hand rough records to a CPA at tax time. It costs more at tax time, but it's survivable. Running a field service business on QuickBooks alone means scheduling in your head or on paper. That approach caps your capacity at maybe five or six jobs a day for a solo operator. You'll lose jobs, double-book yourself, and miss follow-ups. That's revenue leaving every single week.</p>

<h2>The Jobber-QuickBooks Integration</h2>

<p><strong>Jobber and QuickBooks Online integrate natively with two-way sync. Invoices, payments, and client data flow between the two platforms automatically. The integration is available on the Connect plan ($129/mo) and above. It works well enough for most businesses, though you'll occasionally see sync errors that need manual cleanup.</strong></p>

<p>When you send an invoice in Jobber, it pushes to QuickBooks automatically. Payments recorded in Jobber sync to QuickBooks as income. Client records stay in sync between the two systems. You're not doing double data entry every day, which was the standard nightmare before this integration existed.</p>

<p>The sync is not perfect. Errors happen occasionally, usually around tax categories or when a client has duplicate records across the two platforms. Nothing that breaks the business, but plan for a monthly cleanup hour with your bookkeeper. Budget that time into the cost of running both tools together.</p>

<p>One note worth flagging: the QuickBooks sync is only available on Jobber's Connect plan ($129/mo) and above. On Jobber Core, you're manually exporting and importing data, which eliminates most of the operational benefit. If integrating the two is the goal, budget for Connect at minimum.</p>

<h2>Final Verdict: Jobber vs QuickBooks</h2>

<p><strong>These aren't competing products. Jobber runs your field operations. QuickBooks runs your books. Every field service business past the startup phase needs both. If your budget forces a choice: Jobber first, QuickBooks within six months. Running operations cleanly generates more revenue than perfect bookkeeping alone.</strong></p>

<p>QuickBooks is non-negotiable once your business is real. You need clean books, payroll, tax records, and actual margin data. No FSM software replaces it for those functions, and trying to avoid it just creates a bigger mess at tax time that costs more to fix than the subscription would have.</p>

<p>But Jobber is what lets you run more jobs. It stops missed appointments. It gets invoices out the same day instead of three days later. It turns a two-person crew into something that can handle the workload of three people. That operational efficiency translates directly to revenue, and it's the kind of gain that shows up in your bank account within the first month.</p>

<p>Most contractors who switch to Jobber from spreadsheets or paper say the same thing: they can't believe they waited. The scheduling and dispatch features alone tend to justify the cost within the first few weeks of real use.</p>

<p>Use both. Budget for both. If $84/mo combined feels like a stretch, that's a business revenue problem, not a software problem. The answer isn't to cut tools. It's to book more jobs.</p>
<h2>Frequently Asked Questions</h2>
<h3>Q: Which software should I implement first—Jobber or QuickBooks?</h3>
<p>Prioritize Jobber if crew management is your biggest pain point, or QuickBooks if you need payroll and tax tracking. Most field service contractors start with Jobber to handle daily operations, then add QuickBooks as they grow and accounting becomes complex.</p>
<h3>Q: Can QuickBooks handle scheduling and dispatching like Jobber?</h3>
<p>Not effectively. QuickBooks Online has basic job tracking for invoicing, but lacks core field service features like crew scheduling, GPS dispatch, and route optimization. Forcing field operations through QuickBooks creates bottlenecks that Jobber eliminates.</p>
<h3>Q: What's the combined monthly cost of running both Jobber and QuickBooks?</h3>
<p>The minimum is around $84/month (Jobber at $49 + QuickBooks at $35), but most contractors spend $150–$300/month combined for mid-tier plans with more users and features. Many find this cost justified versus the operational overhead of managing crews without proper dispatch software.</p>
<h3>Q: Does Jobber handle payroll and taxes like QuickBooks?</h3>
<p>No. Jobber focuses on field operations and invoicing but doesn't process payroll, handle tax withholding, or generate financial reports—those are QuickBooks' core functions. If payroll is needed, you must use QuickBooks or a dedicated payroll service alongside Jobber.</p>

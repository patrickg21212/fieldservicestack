## Power and Performance

The M18 Fuel delivers 2,000 inch-pounds of torque and 4,000 RPM. The brushless motor runs cooler, lasts longer, and delivers consistent power until the battery dies rather than the gradual fade you get from brushed motors. The 4-speed selector lets you dial back torque for delicate fasteners without switching tools. That alone saves time on finish work or when driving screws into soft material where over-torquing damages the workpiece.

The four speed modes provide genuine versatility that matters in the field:

**Mode 1 (Self-Tapping):** Controlled, low-speed output for machine screws into sheet metal, electrical box mounting, and any fastener where over-driving means starting over. This mode prevents the cam-out and material damage that full-power mode causes on delicate work.

**Mode 2 (Sheet Metal):** Medium speed and torque for general-purpose light fastening. Good for ductwork assembly, cabinet mounting, and mid-range screws where mode 1 is too slow and mode 3 is too aggressive.

**Mode 3 (General Purpose):** The setting you will use 60% of the time. Enough power for most construction fasteners, lag bolts into pilot holes, and general assembly work without the full assault of mode 4.

**Mode 4 (Lag Bolt):** Full power. Drives 3-inch deck screws, removes rusted lag bolts, and handles structural fastening. This is the mode that replaces reaching for a wrench or a larger tool.

The transition between modes is a physical switch on the collar, not a digital setting buried in an app. You change modes with a gloved hand in 2 seconds. This matters more than it sounds like it should. Tools that require phone apps or multi-button sequences to change settings do not get their settings changed in the field.

## Compact Size and Ergonomics

Weight is 2.1 pounds bare tool. With a compact 2.0 Ah battery, you are under 3 pounds. With the 5.0 Ah high-output battery, it adds weight but runs significantly longer between charges. For a full day of work, carry both sizes and swap based on the task. The 2.0 Ah for overhead work and tight spaces. The 5.0 Ah for heavy driving sessions.

The compact form factor fits into tight spaces that larger impact drivers cannot reach. Between joists, behind panels, and inside equipment cabinets, the shorter nose and narrower head profile make a noticeable difference. <a href="/guides/field-service-management-buyers-guide">Field service work</a> involves working in awkward positions constantly. The difference between a driver that fits between a joist and a wall and one that does not is the difference between driving a fastener from a comfortable position and contorting your body to reach from the other side.

The 1/4-inch hex quick-release chuck accepts standard bits without wobble. The built-in LED with a delay-off illuminates the work area for a few seconds after you release the trigger, which is a small detail that proves someone at Milwaukee actually uses their own tools. When you are driving a screw inside a dark cabinet, the LED lights the target. When you release the trigger to check your work, the LED stays on long enough to inspect the result. No need for a headlamp or a second hand holding a flashlight.

The grip texture and balance are well-engineered for extended use. The rubber overmold on the grip prevents the hand fatigue that hard-shell drivers cause after a few hours. The weight distribution keeps the tool from feeling nose-heavy, which matters during overhead work where a front-heavy driver wears out your wrist.

## Battery Life

Battery life with the 5.0 Ah pack lasts a full day of moderate use. Heavy continuous driving will drain it faster, but for the typical mix of tasks on a service call, one battery is usually enough. The M18 platform's cross-compatibility means your batteries work across drills, saws, lights, and everything else in the Milwaukee ecosystem.

The battery ecosystem is Milwaukee's strongest competitive advantage. If you already own M18 tools, adding the impact driver as a bare tool ($149) is significantly cheaper than buying into a new platform. A single set of batteries powers your entire tool collection.

Charge time on the standard M18 charger is roughly 60 to 90 minutes for a 5.0 Ah battery. The M18 rapid charger cuts that to about 45 minutes. For field service work where you cannot afford downtime, carry two batteries and rotate them. One charges in the van while you use the other.

The fuel gauge on M18 batteries shows remaining charge in 4 LED increments. It is not precise, but it prevents the surprise of a dead battery mid-fastener. A quick glance before starting a series of drives tells you whether to swap batteries or push through.

## Durability

The body has taken multiple drops onto concrete from waist height without issue. The rubber overmold on the grip shows wear but holds up. After a year of daily use in dusty, wet, and cold conditions, the tool still performs like it did out of the box. The brushless motor has no carbon brushes to replace, which eliminates the most common maintenance item on impact drivers.

The brushless motor deserves specific emphasis for field service use. Brushed motors degrade over time as carbon brushes wear down. Replacement is straightforward but requires downtime and a trip to the service center or ordering parts. Brushless motors have no wearable internal components. The motor in a brushless impact driver should outlast the tool body itself.

Water resistance is adequate for normal field conditions. Rain exposure, wet hands, and humid environments do not cause issues. Full submersion is not recommended and will void the warranty, but the tool handles real-world job site conditions without babying.

Milwaukee's 5-year warranty covers defects. Their service network is wide enough that turnaround on warranty claims is reasonable, though with this tool, the likelihood of needing it during the warranty period is low.

## What Could Be Better

The bit holder on the side of the tool is a simple friction fit that loses bits in a tool bag. It is a minor complaint but a persistent one. An aftermarket magnetic bit holder is worth the $5 investment. Milwaukee has not addressed this in recent model updates, which suggests they either do not consider it a problem or cannot solve it without redesigning the housing.

The price. The bare tool runs around $149 and a kit with battery, charger, and case pushes $250 to $300. That is premium pricing, though competitive with DeWalt's equivalent 20V MAX offering. If you are already on the M18 platform, the cost is easier to absorb since you can buy the bare tool and use existing batteries.

No belt clip is included. The bare tool ships without a clip, which is an odd omission for a tool designed for field use. The clip is available separately or included in kit versions. Budget an extra $5 to $8 for a clip if you buy the bare tool.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Is the Milwaukee M18 impact driver worth the price over cheaper options?

Yes, if you use it daily. The brushless motor's longevity, 4-speed versatility, and build quality mean the M18 Fuel outlasts cheaper alternatives. A $79 brushed impact driver might last 1 to 2 years of daily field use before the brushes wear out or the motor loses power. The M18 Fuel at $149 bare tool lasts 5+ years with no motor maintenance. On a per-year basis, the premium tool is cheaper. For occasional DIY use, the price premium is harder to justify.

### What battery size should I use with the M18 impact driver?

For field service work, carry both the 2.0 Ah compact and the 5.0 Ah high-output. Use the 2.0 Ah for overhead work, tight spaces, and light fastening where weight matters. Use the 5.0 Ah for extended driving sessions, heavy fastening, and when you need a full day of runtime without swapping. The 2.0 Ah weighs significantly less and makes the tool more maneuverable in cramped spaces.

### How does the Milwaukee M18 Fuel compare to the DeWalt 20V MAX XR?

The Milwaukee M18 Fuel delivers 175 more inch-pounds of torque (2,000 vs. 1,825), includes a 4-speed selector versus DeWalt's 3-speed, and carries a 5-year warranty versus DeWalt's 3-year. DeWalt is $10 cheaper on the bare tool and has a slightly larger battery ecosystem. In daily use, both tools perform comparably on standard field service tasks. The deciding factor for most technicians is their existing battery platform, not the spec sheet differences.

### Can the Milwaukee M18 impact driver remove lug nuts?

The M18 Fuel 1/4-inch hex impact driver is not designed for lug nut removal. While it has enough torque for some lug nuts, the 1/4-inch hex chuck limits you to hex-shank sockets. For automotive lug nuts, use the Milwaukee M18 Fuel 1/2-inch impact wrench (model 2967-20), which delivers 1,400 ft-lbs of nut-busting torque with a 1/2-inch square drive. Different tool for a different job.

### Does the brushless motor really matter?

Yes, and the difference is practical, not just theoretical. Brushless motors last longer because there are no carbon brushes to wear down. They run cooler, which means consistent power delivery and less thermal stress on internal components. They deliver full torque until the battery is depleted instead of gradually weakening as brushed motors do. For a tool you use every day for years, brushless is the feature that determines whether the tool outlives its warranty period or burns out before it.
